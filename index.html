Four moves
between a question
and a payout.

Anyone can post a problem. Almost no one's problem makes it onto the docket. The screen is brutal on purpose: we interview every submission, throw out the vague and the vanity, and only publish what would still matter if the company didn't exist. The work happens in public. The money sits in escrow until it doesn't have to.

01

Intake interview

A two-hour structured interview with the sponsor. We strip away symptoms until we find the real problem. ~93% of submissions are rejected here.

02

Public posting

The problem goes live with clear success criteria, a named judging panel, and nothing left vague. No "transform our customer experience" briefs. Either the threshold is met or it isn't.

03

Solver bidding

Solvers — individuals, labs, consortia — register interest, propose an approach, and post a refundable deposit. Open work threads are visible. Closed-source solvers can work under NDA.

04

Adjudication & payout

The panel scores against the pre-agreed criteria. Winning solver receives the bounty minus a 4% platform fee. Losing solvers get their deposit back, plus a share if their work informed the winner.

Funding mechanism

Dominant assurance, baked in.

Big problems rarely have a single sponsor. ProblemMarket pools capital across companies that share the problem, using a dominant assurance contract: each pledge is refundable until the bounty threshold is met, and pledgers earn a small bonus if the threshold fails.

The bonus flips the freerider problem. Pledging early is now the rational move whether the pool fills or doesn't — which is exactly why the pool fills. We didn't invent this; Alex Tabarrok did, in 1998. We just operationalize it.

CASE Nº 042 · OPENED 2026.04.08 · OPEN

The FDA doesn't recognise ageing as a disease. Build the case that changes that.

Sponsor: Longevity research consortium · 4 biotech firms, 2 sovereign health funds, 1 academic medical centre · published with legal counsel approval.

The blockage. The FDA has no approved indication for biological ageing. This forces every longevity therapeutic into a downstream disease trial — cardiovascular, diabetes, cancer — which takes longer, costs more, and misses the upstream mechanism entirely. The TAME metformin trial was the first attempt to argue ageing as a trial endpoint. It proved the argument is makeable. No one has closed it.

What "won" looks like. A deliverable package — scientific consensus paper, draft regulatory guidance, and a template Phase II trial design using validated biological age endpoints — that is formally accepted onto an FDA Center for Drug Evaluation and Research advisory agenda within 18 months of submission. The package must hold up to adversarial peer review and be co-signed by a minimum of 40 relevant domain experts.

Success criteria, prereg'd

  • Peer-reviewed consensus paper with ≥40 signatories across geroscience, regulatory science, and bioethics
  • Draft regulatory guidance document reviewed and acknowledged by CDER in writing
  • Template trial design using ≥2 validated biological age endpoints (e.g. GrimAge, DunedinPACE)
  • Published in a Nature- or Science-family journal, or equivalent impact
  • Formal placement on an FDA advisory committee agenda within 18 months

What's already been ruled out

  • Pure advocacy documents without peer-reviewed scientific grounding
  • Approaches requiring an Act of Congress or statutory change (scope too large)
  • TAME-style single-drug proposals (too narrow; a general pathway is required)

What sponsors will provide

  • Legal and regulatory affairs counsel with prior FDA advisory experience
  • Introductions to CDER staff and FDA advisory committee members
  • Funding for data analysis, publication fees, and expert convening costs
  • Platform and co-authorship; all IP and attribution retained by the solver team

ADJUDICATION

How winners are decided.

Every case has a panel of three to five domain experts disclosed before the bounty closes. They have no financial interest in the outcome. Their decision is final.

Panel composition

Each case panel is assembled at the time of listing — before solvers begin working. Panels consist of three to five people: domain experts with no equity relationship to the sponsor, no active solver participation in any open case on the platform, and no prior advisory relationship with ProblemMarket.

Panel members are named publicly. Anonymity is not an option. If a panelist cannot be named, they cannot serve.

Conflict of interest

Every panel member signs a conflict-of-interest declaration at the time of appointment. The declaration covers financial interests, prior professional relationships with the sponsor, and any relationship with registered solvers on the case. Declarations are published alongside the panel list.

If a conflict emerges after appointment, the panelist is replaced. If a conflict is discovered after adjudication, the result is reviewed by the full editorial board. This has happened once. The result was upheld.

Scoring process

Panels score against the pre-registered success criteria only. Each criterion is scored independently on a binary pass/fail basis, then aggregated. Partial credit is not awarded unless the original brief explicitly provides for it.

Panelists score independently before convening. Disagreements above a defined threshold trigger a structured deliberation session, the transcript of which is published in redacted form.

Appeals

A solver may request a structured review within 14 days of a result. The review is conducted by a separate two-person panel who did not participate in the original adjudication. The review can only evaluate whether the scoring process followed the pre-registered criteria — it cannot re-weigh the criteria themselves.

The review panel's finding is final. ProblemMarket does not arbitrate beyond this point.

Consolation shares

Where a losing solver's documented work materially informed the winning solution, the winning solver may voluntarily allocate a consolation share from their bounty. ProblemMarket facilitates but does not mandate this. Consolation shares have been paid in 14 of 89 resolved cases.

Join a panel

We maintain a standing roster of domain experts willing to serve on adjudication panels. Time commitment is typically 12–20 hours per case. Panelists receive a flat honorarium of $8,000–$15,000 depending on case complexity. Contact panel@problemmarket.com to be considered.

READING ROOM

The intake handbook.

Everything you need to know before submitting a problem — what we're looking for, what we're not, and how to present a bottleneck clearly enough that solvers can actually find it.

Why most problem submissions fail

The single most common failure mode: submitting a symptom, not a problem. "Our customer retention is dropping" is not a problem. "We cannot identify, with ≥80% accuracy, which customers will churn before their 90-day mark — and we've exhausted our feature-engineering options on 4 years of clickstream data" is a problem.

ProblemMarket exists for the second kind. If you can't yet write a one-sentence problem statement that includes a measurable threshold and a clear boundary around what you've already ruled out, you're not ready for intake.

What makes a good case

  • Verifiable success criteria. The panel must be able to score a solution objectively. "Better UX" cannot be scored. "Task completion rate ≥92% in a blinded usability test of 200 participants" can be.
  • Real stakes. We target problems where a solution creates $7M+ of value — revenue unlocked, cost avoided, or market created. Below that, the bounty economics don't work.
  • Bounded scope. The problem must be solvable by a solver without access to your entire org. Data, context, and liaisons you can provide; organisational change management you cannot outsource.
  • A willing sponsor. Someone at your organisation must own the problem publicly, approve the brief, and commit to a judging timeline. Anonymity of the sponsor is fine; absenteeism is not.

The dominant assurance contract

ProblemMarket uses a funding mechanism invented by economist Alex Tabarrok in 1998. Here's how it works:

  1. A bounty threshold is set at the time of listing (e.g., $10M).
  2. Funders — companies, funds, government bodies — pledge capital toward that threshold.
  3. If the threshold is met, the pledges are locked into escrow and the bounty activates.
  4. If the threshold is not met by the deadline, all pledges are refunded — plus a small bonus (typically 5–8%) paid out of ProblemMarket's reserve.

The bonus flips the freeloader problem. Normally, companies wait to see if others will fund a public good before pledging. With dominant assurance, pledging early is the dominant strategy whether the pool fills or not. Early pledgers earn a guaranteed return if the pool fails. This is why pools fill.

Panel members sign a conflict-of-interest declaration and are bound by the same NDA structure as solvers. The panel's decision is final; ProblemMarket does not adjudicate disputes beyond a one-time structured review.

ABOUT PROBLEMA

What we are.

A marketplace for the world's most expensive unsolved problems. Not a consultancy. Not a prize foundation. Something in between — with a mechanism that makes it work when both of those usually don't.

The premise

Most hard organisational problems go unsolved not because no one can solve them, but because the right solver never finds out they exist. Big companies publish RFPs that are too vague to act on. Prize competitions attract the wrong people and take years to close. Consultants get hired to deliver reports, not answers.

ProblemMarket makes the problem the product. We publish it, price it, and hold the escrow until someone solves it to a standard that a neutral panel has pre-agreed to recognise.

How we're structured

ProblemMarket Holdings operates the marketplace and takes a 4% fee from winning solver payouts. We do not take equity. We do not retain IP. We do not consult for sponsors. The editorial board — which decides what gets listed — has no sponsors on it. The board of directors has no solvers on it.

We operate from Tampa, Oslo, and New York. The platform launched in 2024. Since then, $214M has been dispatched to 89 winning solver entities across 31 industries.

Editorial independence

We decline problems from companies we have financial relationships with. We publish the judging panel before the bounty closes so sponsors cannot influence the outcome by threatening to withdraw. Our editorial standards are published and updated publicly.

If a sponsor asks us to remove a listing after it goes live, we decline. The docket is a public record. The only exception is a court order, and we publish those too.
